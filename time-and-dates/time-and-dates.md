# Dates & Time (日付と時間)

Japanese dates use numbers combined with specific counters. However, there are many irregular readings, especially for days of the month!

> [!WARNING]
> **Cultural Note: The Unlucky Number 4 (shi)**  
> You might wonder why we often avoid reading the number 4 as **shi**. In Japanese, *shi* (四) sounds exactly like the word for **death** (死). Because of this deep superstition, Japanese people generally prefer the reading **yon** for four. However, in certain fixed time counters like April (*shi-gatsu*) and 4 o'clock (*yo-ji*), we still use variations of the older readings!

## Months (月 - gatsu)

For months, simply add **月 (gatsu)** to the number. 
*Note the highlighted irregularities for April, July, and September!*

### Putting It Together: Full Dates

In Japanese, dates are written from largest to smallest: **Month + Day**.
Here are some examples (including the tricky ones!):

| Date | Kanji | Romaji / Reading |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **February 3** (2-3) | 二月三日 | ni-gatsu mikka |
| **February 14** (2-14)| 二月十四日 | ni-gatsu jū-yokka |
| **April 1** (4-1) | 四月一日 | shi-gatsu tsuitachi |

## Time (時間)

### Hours (時 - ji)

To state the time, add **時 (ji)** to the number. 
*Note the highlighted irregularities for 4 o'clock, 7 o'clock, and 9 o'clock!*

| Number | Kanji | Romaji |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | 一時 | ichi-ji |
| 2 | 二時 | ni-ji |
| 3 | 三時 | san-ji |
| **4** | **四時** | **yo-ji** *(not yon!)* |
| 5 | 五時 | go-ji |
| 6 | 六時 | roku-ji |
| **7** | **七時** | **shichi-ji** |
| 8 | 八時 | hachi-ji |
| **9** | **九時** | **ku-ji** *(not kyū!)* |
| 10 | 十時 | jū-ji |
| 11 | 十一時 | jū-ichi-ji |

### Minutes (分 - fun / pun)

Minutes are tricky because the counter **分** changes its pronunciation (**fun** vs **pun** / **ppun**) depending on the number before it.

### Quick Grouping Cheat Sheet

| Sound | Numbers | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **fun** | 2, 5, 7, 9 | ni-fun (2), go-fun (5) |
| **pun** | 3, 4 | san-pun (3), yon-pun (4) |
| **ppun** | 1, 6, 8, 10 | ippun (1), roppun (6) |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> A common shortcut for expressing "30 minutes" (half past the hour) is using **半 (han)**. 
> For example: **4:30** is **四時半 (yo-ji han)**.

| Number | Kanji | Romaji | Pronunciation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **一分** | **ippun** | **ppun** |
| 2 | 二分 | ni-fun | fun |
| **3** | **三分** | **san-pun** | **pun** |
| **4** | **四分** | **yon-pun** | **pun** |
| 5 | 五分 | go-fun | fun |
| **6** | **六分** | **roppun** | **ppun** |
| 7 | 七分 | nana-fun | fun |
| **8** | **八分** | **happun** | **ppun** |
| 9 | 九分 | kyū-fun | fun |
| **10** | **十分** | **juppun** / **jippun** | **ppun** |

*(For numbers above 10, just combine them! E.g., **25 min** = ni-jū-go-fun. **11 min** = jū-ippun).*


> [!TIP]
> **12-Hour vs. 24-Hour Clock**  
> Japan uses both! In everyday conversation, the **12-hour clock** is most common (using **gozen** / **gogo**). However, for train schedules, formal announcements, and television, the **24-hour clock** is standard. 
> 
> | Time | 12-Hour Clock (Everyday) | 24-Hour Clock (Formal) |
> | :--- | :--- | :--- |
> | **8:00 AM** | 午前八時 (*gozen hachi-ji*) | 八時 (*hachi-ji*) |
> | **2:00 PM** | 午後二時 (*gogo ni-ji*) | 十四時 (*jū-yon-ji*) |
> | **5:00 PM** | 午後五時 (*gogo go-ji*) | 十七時 (*jū-shichi-ji*) |
> | **8:00 PM** | 午後八時 (*gogo hachi-ji*) | 二十時 (*ni-jū-ji*) |

### Putting It Together: Full Times

Combining AM/PM, Hours, and Minutes is just like English: **AM/PM + Hour + Minute**.

| Time | Kanji | Romaji / Reading |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **8:34 AM** | 午前八時三十四分 | gozen hachi-ji san-jū-yon-pun |
| **12:26 PM** | 午後十二時二十六分 | gogo jū-ni-ji ni-jū-roppun |
| **7:30** | 七時半 | shichi-ji han |
